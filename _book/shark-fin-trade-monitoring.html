12.1 Molecular Ecology & Conservation Genetics

The central question of ecology is to understand how organisms interact with each other and their environment and the resulting observed patterns of distribution and abundance. The field of molecular ecology has arisen as a reaction to the limited ability to observe certain biological processes in situ and directly obtain data. Instead, molecular genetics techniques and population genetics theory are applied to understand questions in ecology, evolution, behavior, and conservation biology. This toolkit includes a variety of approaches, both in terms of available genetic markers and the analytic methods used to analyze them.

One of the central paradigms of molecular ecology is the assumption that we can use signals in genomic data sets to understand the ecology and demographic history of populations because evolutionary forces shape the patterns of within and between group patterns of genetic diversity - the specific application of these methods to small populations is studied in the field of Conservation Genetics.

We can also use genetics to characterize and identify (groups of) individuals (DNA fingerprinting), populations (mixed stock analysis), and species (Genetic Barcoding).

Genetic barcoding is a technique that is increasingly used for wildlife forensics because it allows identification of unknown and frequently degraded samples that cannot be morphologically identified. Under certain circumstances, it can also be used to identify the geographic region of origin.

Today, we will learn how to use genetic markers (barcodes) to be able to distinguish between species and identify unknown specimen for a variety of tissue and other samples. We are going to apply this in the context of monitoring the trade of shark fins in the Guangzhou (mainland China) market. This data set has been published as

Cardeñosa et al. (2020). Species composition of the largest shark fin retail-market in mainland China. Scientific Reports, 10(1), 1-10.

the lastest study in a series of paper using genetic barcoding to understand the species composition of the Hong Kong shark fin market:

  • Clarke et al. (2006). Identification of shark species composition and proportion in the Hong Kong shark fin market based on molecular genetics and trade records. Conservation Biology, 20(1), 201-211.
  • Clarke et al. (2005). Estimates of shark species composition and numbers associated with the shark fin trade based on Hong Kong auction data. Journal of Northwest Atlantic Fishery Science, 35, 453-465.
  • Fields et al. (2018). Species composition of the international shark fin trade assessed through a retail‐market survey in Hong Kong. Conservation Biology, 32(2), 376-389.

12.2 Genetic Barcoding

Genetic barcoding allows for identification of an unknown specimen for which DNA can be obtained using a short section of DNA using a specific gene or genes. To be able to do this you need a gene that is present in all (or a large group of species), flanked by regions that are conserved across a wide range of species so that universal primers can be designed. Further, the gene must be variable among species but conserved within a species.

Barcoding is widely applied * conservation efforts * identify/stop illegal trading (ivory, shark fins) * food fraud (proper labeling, fish/sushi) * identify life stages otherwise hard to identify (larval stage of insects, amphibians, fish)

Genetic barcoding usually consists of these steps:

  1. Obtain sample
  2. Extract DNA
  3. Perform PCR using universal primers to amplify barcoding gene (for animals usually COI)
  4. Sequence amplified gene
  5. Compare sequence to reference database to get sample ID

12.3 Shark Fins/fishery

Shark fin soup is considered a delicacy in Chinese culture and is generally served as a luxury item at special occasions, including weddings and banquets. The taste itself comes from other ingredients while the shark fins provide texture.

Increasing wealth of the middle class increased demand for fins resulting in an increase in shark fin prices. As a result, the practice of shark fining became increasingly lucrative. Because the fins were so much more valuable than the meat, the fins were cut off of sharks that were caught and the carcasses tossed back into the water. Ethical concerns compounded with concerns over unsustainable shark fishing resulted in public outcry and campaigns to ban fining and in the some countries there has been increasing pressure for restaurants to stop serving shark fin soup completely.

In the late 90s and early 2000s, fisheries-driven declines of some shark species were linked to the fin trade. Despite the fin trade being a valuable global trade, monitoring it on a species-specific basis is difficult as few countries with commercial or subsistence shark fisheries keep accurate records or regulate specific shark fisheries. As a result, it can be difficult to quantify the impact of the fin trade on shark populations and their declines.

12.4 How to use blast to identify DNA sequences

Your data folder contains a file called shark_fins.fasta. A fasta file is a file format for storing genetic information it consists of a header line in the format >name followed be the sequence in the subsequent line(s). A multi-fasta file like the one in your data folder contains multiple sequences, each sequence has its own header line with unique identifiers.

Genbank is a “comprehensive public database of nucleotide sequences and supporting bibliographical and biological annotation”. We can use the Basic Local Alignment Tool, BLAST to compare our sequences to COI sequences that have been deposited in genbank.

To do this go to the BLAST website. Here, you can choose a BLAST program to run. We will be doing a nucleotide blast.

Now, open the multi-fasta file - you can do this by clicking on it in the file navigation pane (bottom left), it will then open in your Viewer pane in Rstudio (top right). Select a random sequence, highlight and copy it.

Now, in blast paste your sequence into the box Enter Query Sequence.

Next, select the database to be searched. Select Nucleotide Collection (nr/nt) to identify an unknown sequence.

In general, your Program Selection will be based on the goal of your search. Here, you are attempting to identify an unknown sample – use highly similar sequences (megablast). Other options are more dissimilar sequences (discontinuous megablast) to be used to find closely related species and somewhat similar sequences (blastn) to be used for short (<20bp) searches.

Finally, click the BLAST button to initiate your query. A BLAST search is a pairwise comparison between the DNA sequence you have uploaded and all the DNA sequences deposited in Genbank.

Within a few seconds, you will receive a BLAST report once the search is completed. It consists of a header giving you the basic information on the sequence you uploaded, a graphical overview and a list of your search results detailing the Accession number, species ID and scores & statistics ranking your results.

Your results are ranked by the Max Score/Total score which scores the pairwise comparison between your DNA sequence and the search results. Higher scores mean better alignments. Further, you will receive and e-value which indicates if the results would be expected by chance alone. The lower the E-value the less likely the results will be obtained by chance.

For more information on your query results select a result by clicking on the hyperlink.

The detailed description contains information on the Accession number (a unique identifier code for a sequence), a description of the species, BLAST scores & Percent identity and a Pairwise Alignment of your query DNA sequence and the result.

For even more information on the sequence it has been matched to, you can clikc on the accession number

The pairwise alignment compares your query (unknown) sequence to the subject (result) sequence. Your query will generally begin at nucleotide 1 though the best matching subject sequence might not.
