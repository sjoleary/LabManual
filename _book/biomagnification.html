10 Biomagnification

Date: 9/30/2020

Learning Objectives

After completing this lab you should

  • be able to list the primary players in a food web according to their trophic level.
  • understand what the “10%-rule” is for loss of energy between trophic levels.
  • understand the concept of biomagnification.

Food webs can be used to summarize species interactions (consumption, competition) as well as trophic interactions, including the transfer of energy between trophic levels and the recycling of nutrients through the system. Both energy and matter are conserved per se, i.e. they are neihter created nor destroyed though they do take different routes through the ecosystem. Nutrients are recycled while, energy flows through the system in a unidirectional fashion, generally entering as light and exiting as heat.

Organisms can be categorized by their role in the ecosystem according to their trophic levels as primary producers, primary, secondary, tertiary consumers1 generally consumers at the highest trophic level are designated as top predators, and decomposers.

Energy usually enters an ecosystem as sunlight and is captured in chemical form by primary producers through photosynthesis. This is called energy fixation. This energy is now available to other organisms in the the ecosystem. As the energy travels up the trophic chains it changes forms as organisms consume other species, metabolize biomass, and eventually die.

Each time an organism is consumed, only a portion of the energy in the prey is actually incorporated into the biomass of the consumer - the rest of the energy is “burned off” during metabolism or passes through the consumer undigested. This process of energy loss occurs at each trophic level. As a rule of thumb, approximately 10% of energy consumed is used for growth/biomass production and thus made available for organisms at the next trophic level. This also results in an decrease of biomass with increasing trophic levels. In reality, the efficiency of energy transfer, i.e. how much energy is transferred to the next trophic level and how much energy dissipates as heat during cellular respiration varies depending on the proportion of organisms that die without being eaten, how much of a food item is not digested (and therefor not used). So energy does not “disappear” it just winds up as heat instead of being available to the ecosystem.

Heavy metals, some pesticides, and other organic compounds are not broken down during consumption and are incorporated into the biomass of the consumer. This means that the amount of the compound remains the same - though the amount of biomass it is “diluted in” becomes increasingly smaller as it “travels” up the food chain. In addition, it does not break down, so as an organisms consume food containing this type of compound they will retain all the compounds they eat.

This process is called biomagnification or bioaccumulation. Rachel Carson’s 1962 book “Silent Spring” was one of the first times this concept was illustrated for the general public as she described how DDT in near-shore waters accumulates across trophic levels of a food web resulting in bird population being decimated by thinning egg-shells.

Now, we can analyze our results. First, we will plot changes in current DDT concentration over time.

# read data
biomagn <- read_delim("data/biomagnification.txt", delim = "\t")

# make tidy data set to plot facets
tidy <- biomagn %>%
  pivot_longer(cols = 4:8, names_to = "ORGANISM", values_to = "CONCENTRATION")

# plot results
ggplot(tidy, aes(x = TIME, y = CONCENTRATION, color = ORGANISM)) +
  geom_line() +
  geom_point() +
  facet_grid(CONC_WATER ~ .) +
  labs(x = "time", y = "DDT [ppm]") +
  theme(legend.position = "bottom")

Next, let’s compare the ratios among adjacent trophic levels.

# calculate ratios
ratios <- biomagn %>%
  mutate(wat_phyto = phytoplankton/CONC_WATER,
         phyto_zoop = zooplankton/phytoplankton,
         zoopl_smelt = smelt/zooplankton,
         smelt_salm = salmon/smelt,
         salm_peli = pelicans/smelt,
         smelt_peli = pelicans/smelt) %>%
  select(-phytoplankton, -zooplankton, -smelt, -salmon, -pelicans)

# create tidy data set to plot
tidy <- ratios %>%
  pivot_longer(cols = 4:9, names_to = "RATIO", values_to = "VALUES")

# plot ratios over time
ggplot(tidy, aes(x = TIME, y = VALUES, color = RATIO)) +
  geom_line() +
  geom_point() +
  facet_grid(RATIO ~ CONC_WATER, scales = "free") +
  labs(x = "time", y = "ratio DDT concentration") +
  theme(legend.position = "bottom")
